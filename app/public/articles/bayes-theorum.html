Conditional, Joint, and Marginal Probabilities

Three types of probability! What's the difference?

Joint probability: this is referring to the probability of two events happening at the same time. If you have a deck of cards, asking the question "what is the probability of drawing a queen of hearts" is a joint probability question - a queen and a heart.

P(Queen and Heart)=152P(\text{Queen and Heart}) = \frac{1}{52}P(Queen and Heart)=521​

Marginal probability: the probability of a event happening, regardless of the outcomes of the other events. What is the probability of drawing a heart?

P(Heart)=1352=14P(\text{Heart}) = \frac{13}{52} = \frac{1}{4}P(Heart)=5213​=41​

Conditional probability: the probability of an event occurring, given another event already occurred. If you draw a card, and it is a queen, what's the probability of getting another? That is to say, the probability of drawing two queens in a row? 51 cards remaining, only 3 queens. So:

P(Draw another queen | Draw a queen)=351≈5.8%P(\text{Draw another queen | Draw a queen}) = \frac{3}{51} \approx 5.8\%P(Draw another queen | Draw a queen)=513​≈5.8%

Formulas

There are concise and clear formulas for each of these. To illustrate, let's use dice. Let's say there are two dices, D1D_1D1​ and D2D_2D2​. We deliberately choose D1D_1D1​. The conditional probabilty of rolling iii with D1D_1D1​ is:

P(i∣D1)P(i|D_1)P(i∣D1​)

It's the conditional probability - given an event (picking D1D_1D1​ out of two dice).

If, instead of deliberately choosing D1D_1D1​, we choose a dice at random (out of two dices). Let's represent the selected dice as DjD_jDj​, where j is 1 or 2. The probability of selecting dice is P(Dj)P(D_j)P(Dj​). So joint probability of selecting a specific dice jjj and rolling a specific number iii is the product of the probabilities, P(i,Dj)P(i,D_j)P(i,Dj​).

P(i,Dj)=P(Dj)∗P(i∣Dj)P(i,D_j) = P(D_j) * P(i|D_j)P(i,Dj​)=P(Dj​)∗P(i∣Dj​)

Let's work it out.

Probability of a dice, jjj, out of two candidates:

P(Dj)=0.5P(D_j) = 0.5P(Dj​)=0.5

And of getting iii, given DjD_jDj​:

P(i,Dj)=16P(i,D_j) = \frac{1}{6}P(i,Dj​)=61​

So simply

0.5∗16=1120.5 * \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{12}0.5∗61​=121​

Intuitively, this makes sense - two dice times 6 sides gives us 12 outcomes.

Generalizing

From this example, we can generalize. Let's look at this formula:

P(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)∗P(Y)P(X, Y) = P(X|Y) * P(Y)P(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)∗P(Y)

To be ultra specific:

  • P(X,Y)P(X,Y)P(X,Y) is the joint probability of event XXX and event YYY happening together. The comma means "together".
  • P(X∣Y)P(X|Y)P(X∣Y) is the conditional probability. The probability XXX will occur, given observation YYY. The pipe symbol means conditional.
  • P(Y)P(Y)P(Y) is the marginal probability. It is the probability of YYY happening on its own.

See the parallel between the formula we developed by intuition for the dice example?

Dice example:

P(i,Dj)=P(Dj)∗P(i∣Dj)P(i,D_j) = P(D_j) * P(i|D_j)P(i,Dj​)=P(Dj​)∗P(i∣Dj​)

Formal formula:

P(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)∗P(Y)P(X, Y) = P(X|Y) * P(Y)P(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)∗P(Y)

Example

Here is an applied example!

Consider an occasionally dishonest casino that uses two kinds of dice. Of the dice 99% are fair but 1% are loaded so that a six comes up 50% of the time. We pick up a die from a table at random.

What are P(six∣Dloaded)P(six|D_{loaded})P(six∣Dloaded​) and P(six∣Dfair)P(six|D_{fair})P(six∣Dfair​)?

What are P(six,Dloaded)P(six, D_{loaded})P(six,Dloaded​) and P(six,Dfair)P(six, D_{fair})P(six,Dfair​)?

What is the probability of rolling a six from the die we picked up?

Let's start with the first one:

What are P(six∣Dloaded)P(six|D_{loaded})P(six∣Dloaded​) and P(six∣Dfair)P(six|D_{fair})P(six∣Dfair​)?

Note the ∣|∣. This is a conditional probability question. P(six∣Dloaded)P(six|D_{loaded})P(six∣Dloaded​) - in English "Given a loaded dice, what is the probability of rolling a 6?" We are told - it's 50%, or 0.5.

P(six∣Dfair)P(six|D_{fair})P(six∣Dfair​) is, of course, 16\frac{1}{6}61​

The next two are subtly different:

What are P(six,Dloaded)P(six, D_{loaded})P(six,Dloaded​) and P(six,Dfair)P(six, D_{fair})P(six,Dfair​)?

There is no ∣|∣ - this is a joint probability question. It is asking the chance of these two things happening together.

Let's start with P(six,Dloaded)P(six, D_{loaded})P(six,Dloaded​). We need to know:

  • chance of getting a loaded dice
  • chance of getting a 6

Intuitively, this should be a low number - it's unlikely to get a loaded die, and even if you do, there is still only a 50% chance to throw a 6.

We can use the formula from earlier:

P(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)∗P(Y)P(X, Y) = P(X|Y) * P(Y)P(X,Y)=P(X∣Y)∗P(Y)

We know the 99% of dice are fair but 1% are loaded. Of those, they come up 6 50% of the time.

P(six,Dloaded)=P(six∣Dloaded)∗P(Dloaded)P(six, D_{loaded}) = P(six|D_{loaded}) * P(D_{loaded})P(six,Dloaded​)=P(six∣Dloaded​)∗P(Dloaded​)
P(six,Dloaded)=12∗1100P(six, D_{loaded}) = \frac{1}{2} * \frac{1}{100}P(six,Dloaded​)=21​∗1001​
=0.5∗0.01=0.005=0.5%= 0.5 * 0.01 = 0.005 = 0.5\%=0.5∗0.01=0.005=0.5%

Less than 1% chance.

What is the probability of rolling a six from the die we picked up?

This is asking about marginal probability. It is asking for the outcome considering all possibilities. For this, we use the law of total probability. It's the sum of all probabilities or all "paths".

P(X)=∑i=1nP(X∣Y)P(Y)P(X) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(X|Y) P(Y)P(X)=i=1∑n​P(X∣Y)P(Y)

It's the formula from before P(X∣Y)P(Y)P(X|Y) P(Y)P(X∣Y)P(Y), just with a ∑i=1n\sum_{i=1}^n∑i=1n​ in front!

We need to know:

  • chance to get 6 on a loaded dice
  • chance to get 6 on a fair dice
P(X)=P(6∣Dloaded)∗P(Dloaded)+P(6∣Dfair)∗P(Dfair)P(X) = P(6|D_{loaded}) * P(D_{loaded}) + P(6|D_{fair}) * P(D_{fair})P(X)=P(6∣Dloaded​)∗P(Dloaded​)+P(6∣Dfair​)∗P(Dfair​)

This is:

P(X)=0.5∗0.01+16∗0.99=0.005+0.166=0.17\begin{aligned} P(X) & = 0.5 * 0.01 + \frac{1}{6} * 0.99 \\ & = 0.005 + 0.166 \\ & = 0.17 \end{aligned}P(X)​=0.5∗0.01+61​∗0.99=0.005+0.166=0.17​

Odds are about 17%.
